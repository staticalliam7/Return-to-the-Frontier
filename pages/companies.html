ASA or the Australian Space Agency, is an Australian state backed space agency funded and ran by CEO Kadam Kilmour, with no launch vehicles yet announced, but one planned to be announced early 2006/late 2005

Founded in 1969, the CRSP (中华民国太空计划) was to help China catch up to the West and the Communists in space technology. Over the course of a decade, the CRSP played an instrumental role in spaceflight while the Republic of China gained power and prestige. It established communications with extraterrestrials, landed on Mars, and has created several bases, including the largest moon base (Chengjiu City) and the largest space station (Tiantangmen).

KASA has launched many notable payloads, including the Hecate Spacecraft (First Spacecraft to perform a crewed flyby of the moon.), the Sojoiurner Spacecraft (First Spacecraft to land crew on mars.), the Intrepid Spacecraft and many other notable robotic missions.

Saab AB (originally Svenska Aeroplan AB, later just SAAB and Saab Group) is a Swedish aerospace and defence company, founded in 1937. Headquartered in Stockholm, the development and the manufacturing is undertaken in Linköping. Saab produced automobiles from 1947 until 1990 when the automobile division was spun off as Saab Automobile, a joint venture with General Motors. The joint venture ended in 2000 when GM took complete ownership. From 1968 onwards the company was in a merger with commercial vehicle manufacturer Scania-Vabis, known as Saab-Scania. The two were de-merged in 1995 by the new owners, Investor AB. Despite the demerger, both Saab and Scania share the right to use the griffin logo, which originates from the coat of arms of the Swedish region of Scania.
SAAB currently is developing the SLS (Swedish Launch Vehicle) LV.

BAE Systems was founded in 1995 after a merger between British Aerospace and Marconi Electronic Systems, as the successor to many other prestigious British aeronautical corporations. Through government defense contracts manufacturing aircraft, naval vessels and weaponry, they branched out into the commercial launch market, something that was already planned by British Aerospace before the merger. British Aerospace had designed the British Satellite Launch Vehicle (BSLV-1) which could carry payloads of over 500kg to a low Earth orbit. Because Britain didn't have it's own launch centers, the vehicle launched from the Wallops Space Launch Center and the Kennedy Space Center, making it's first flight in 1997. With an impressive flight cadence of 5 launches a year, owing it to the two operational launch sites and easy construction methods, BAE was able to rack up 22 successful launches of the BSLV-1 between 1997 and 2001.
